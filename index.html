12-Week Home Program

A family guide to support your child's communication at home, every day — to be used alongside speech pathology sessions.

Quick start guide

Read this first — everything you need to know before you begin.

What this program is for

This program gives you tools to support your child's communication during everyday routines at home.

What to do each day

  • During the day: Use the strategies naturally in daily routines, such as meals, bath time, play, and getting dressed.
  • One activity per day: Do one 15–30 minute activity from the Activities section. Alternate between preferred activities, such as slime, sensory play, or ASMR, and more structured activities.
  • After the activity: Fill in the activity log. This takes 2–3 minutes.
  • End of day: Tick the daily recap. This takes 1–2 minutes.
  • End of week: Fill in the weekly reflection. This takes about 5 minutes.

Important things to remember

  • Follow your child's lead. If she is not interested, try again another time.
  • Avoid correcting her language. Instead, model a clearer or more helpful phrase calmly, then move on.
  • If she is upset or dysregulated, pause or stop the activity and go to the "If upset" section.
  • Missing a day is okay. Note it briefly and keep going.
  • If something does not feel right for your child or your family, tell your speech pathologist.
  • Bring your completed activity logs and reflection sheets to each speech pathology session.

A note on language at home

You may speak more than one language at home — this is completely fine and is good for your child's development. You can use this program in whichever language feels most natural for each situation. If you have questions about which language to use for specific activities, please ask your speech pathologist.

Program timeline: 12 weeks total. Formal reviews at Weeks 4, 8, and 12. At each review your speech pathologist will look at your data sheets, discuss what is working, and may adjust the program. Nothing in this program is fixed — everything can be changed if it is not right for your family.

Communication goals

These goals are functional, measurable, specific, and linked to your child's everyday communication needs. Please review and confirm them with your speech pathologist before starting. They may be adjusted at any check-in.

⭐ Primary goal
Across everyday home routines, your child will use short, meaningful 1–3 word messages to request, comment, protest, ask for help, or share needs — with increasing spontaneity and flexibility.
Examples: "I want slime," "more please," "open," "my turn," "help," "I'm done," "bath time," "I feel tired"
How we will know she is reaching this goal:
  • She uses 1–3 word messages in at least 3 different everyday routines or activities, such as play, meals, bath time, getting dressed, or transitions
  • Messages are used for a clear communication purpose, such as requesting, refusing, commenting, asking for help, or sharing a need
  • Record whether each message was spontaneous (no prompt) or prompted — both count, but spontaneous use should increase over time
  • Progress is reviewed using activity logs and daily recaps across 4 consecutive weeks
Clinician note: Please confirm the expected frequency after baseline observation so the target is realistic for the child and family.
Supporting goal 1
Your child will communicate a feeling using a word, approximation, gesture, or visual support in at least 2 different situations per week.
Examples: "sad," "angry," "tired," "I feel calm," pointing to an emotion face card, or using gesture + word.
How we will know:
  • Any functional form of emotion communication counts — spoken word, approximation, gesture + word, or pointing to the emotion card
  • Record the situation and whether it was spontaneous or prompted in the daily recap or activity log
  • Progress is shown when she begins to use emotion words or visuals in more than one routine, not only during structured practice
Supporting goal 2
Your child will communicate at least one body need per day using speech, approximation, gesture, or visual support.
Examples: "hungry," "thirsty," "toilet," "hurts," "tired," "need squeeze," pointing to the body needs card, or showing a body part.
How we will know:
  • Tick in the daily recap each time a body need is communicated — any functional form counts
  • Note whether she initiated it or whether you prompted, and what she used: word, visual, gesture, or combination
  • From Week 5 onward, aim to see more spontaneous body-need communication across daily routines
Supporting goal 3
Your child will use varied or flexible language in at least 2 familiar daily routines per week, rather than relying only on the same fixed script.
Examples: changing part of a script, using a single useful word from a script, or using a new short phrase in a familiar activity.
How we will know:
  • Note in the weekly reflection any time she says something new or different in a familiar situation — even a small change counts
  • Record the routine, what she said or did, and whether it was spontaneous or prompted
  • Progress is shown by at least 2 examples of varied language per week across familiar routines
These goals are designed to support meaningful communication in daily life. Progress may be faster or slower than expected — every child develops at their own pace. This program may support communication development when used consistently alongside speech therapy. It does not guarantee a specific outcome.

Understanding your child's language style

This section explains how your child may be learning and using language — and why the activities in this program are designed to be natural, low-pressure, and responsive.

How your child learns language

Many children learn language by building up single words and then combining them, such as learning “I,” “want,” and “slime,” then later saying “I want slime.”

Some children, including many autistic children, use language in bigger chunks or scripts. For example, a child might use a phrase like “Amma I want gimme please” as one whole phrase for requesting. This is often discussed using the term gestalt language processing, and it is closely linked with echolalia.

Echolalia and scripted language are not something to stop or punish. They can be meaningful forms of communication. They may show that your child is using the language she has available to request, protest, comment, join in, or regulate.

Over time, with responsive support, some children begin to use their scripts more flexibly — for example, shortening them, mixing parts of different phrases, or pulling out useful words. This program is designed to support flexible, meaningful communication in everyday routines.

A note about the NLA framework

The stages below are based on the Natural Language Acquisition, or NLA, framework described by Marge Blanc. This framework can be a helpful way to think about scripted language and echolalia, but it should be understood as a clinical guide, not a guaranteed sequence that every child will follow.

Your child appears to be using many whole phrases and some mixed phrases. This program supports her movement toward more flexible communication.

What to avoid during this type of language learning

These activities work best when communication is practised in real, natural, motivating moments.

Try to avoid:

  • asking her to repeat single words in isolation, such as “Say: water”
  • using flashcard drills or word-matching tasks outside of real situations
  • correcting her scripts or telling her the “right” way to say something
  • requiring her to produce a target phrase before she gets what she wants

The activities in this program are designed to keep communication natural, supportive, and low-pressure. If you are unsure whether an activity is appropriate, check with your speech pathologist first.

A note on single words

Single words are okay to model when they are meaningful and linked to the moment.

For example:

  • say “open” while opening something
  • say “more” while giving more
  • say “finished” when something ends
  • say “help” while helping
  • say “slime” when she reaches for slime

This is different from asking her to repeat a word just because an adult says it.

Helpful modelling sounds like:
“Open.”
“More slime.”
“Finished.”
“Help.”
“Slime.”
Less helpful prompting sounds like:
“Say open.”
“What’s this?”
“Say slime first.”
“Tell me the word.”

The goal is not to make her perform a word. The goal is to give her useful language in the moment, so she can learn what the word means and how it helps her communicate.

What echolalia means for this program

Echolalia — repeating words or phrases she has heard — is not something to stop or discourage. It can show that she is processing language and using the language she has available to communicate.

Some echolalia may be her way of requesting, commenting, protesting, joining in, expressing emotion, or regulating. Over time, with supportive modelling and natural communication opportunities, her echolalia may become more flexible and easier for others to understand.

The strategies in this program support this by responding to her communication, modelling useful language, and avoiding pressure to perform.

Everyday strategies

Use these during daily routines — meals, bath, play, getting dressed. Start with 1 or 2 that feel natural. Add more over time.

The 3:1 rule — use more comments than questions

A helpful rule of thumb is to make about three comments for every one question. Comments reduce pressure and give her language to copy. Questions can still be useful, but too many questions can feel like testing and may make communication harder.

🪞

Model and wait

Say the phrase you want her to hear, naturally, then pause for 5–10 seconds. Stay calm and available, but avoid staring or pressuring her. Do not repeat the phrase over and over — just wait.

💡 Say "I want slime," while looking at the slime. Then pause and count silently to 10.
⚠️ Avoid: asking her to repeat what you said.
📣

Expand her language

When she uses a word, gesture, sign, approximation, or phrase, say a slightly longer version back naturally and warmly. Do not ask her to repeat yours.

💡 She says "slime" → you say "I want slime."
⚠️ Avoid: expanding more than one step beyond what she said.
⏸️

Pause and create a gap

Pause during a familiar routine or phrase. Use a warm look, raised eyebrows, or a pause in your action as a signal. Wait to see if she fills the gap with a word, sound, gesture, look, or movement.

💡 "Time for..." [pause] — wait for "bath," "bath time," or any attempt.
⚠️ Avoid: jumping in immediately if she is quiet. Count to 10 first, as long as she is calm.
🎯

Communication opportunities

Create small, playful reasons for her to communicate by pausing briefly during something motivating. Give her time to show you what she wants using any form of communication — words, gestures, reaching, looking, pointing, signs, or visuals.

💡 Hold the slime container and pause before opening it. She may say "open," reach, look at you, or point. Model: "Open." Then open it.
⚠️ Avoid: withholding or waiting too long. If she becomes frustrated, help straight away and model the word as you do: "Open. I'm opening it."
🔀

Offer choices

Give two options instead of yes/no questions. Hold one in each hand, show pictures, or point to visuals so she can choose by looking, pointing, reaching, or talking.

💡 "Slime video or bubbles video?" — hold both options out.
⚠️ Avoid: offering too many choices at once.
🌟

Respond to all communication

Any attempt counts — a look, gesture, point, reach, approximation, sign, visual, or phrase. Respond warmly and naturally. Keep it calm — just do the thing she communicated.

💡 She reaches for more → give it and say "More slime! Here you go."
⚠️ Avoid: over-praising with clapping every time. Calm, natural responses work best.
🗣️

Talk out loud during routines

Narrate what you are doing in short phrases. This gives her language to hear without pressure to respond.

💡 "Washing hands. Soap on. Rub rub. Rinse. All done. Dry hands."
⚠️ Avoid: turning narration into lots of questions. Keep it mostly one-way commentary.
📋

Point to visuals as you speak

Tap the word or picture on the core board or emotion card as you say it. This links the word to its meaning. She does not need to copy you.

💡 Tap "more" on the board while saying "More" — no need for her to point too.
⚠️ Avoid: taking her hand and making her point.
🧲

Follow her interests

If she is engaged in something she loves, that is the best time to practise communication. Use slime, ASMR, sensory play, favourite videos, and small objects as natural opportunities.

💡 Watch a slime video together and comment: "So satisfying. Slime is stretching. I feel calm."
⚠️ Avoid: redirecting her to a different activity just for practice. Go to where she already is.

Prompting guide — how much help to give

Start with the least help and only add more support if she does not respond. The goal over time is for her to need less prompting, so we always begin with the smallest amount of help.

Remember: prompting should feel supportive, not like a test. If she becomes frustrated, help her straight away and model the language as you do it.

Step 1
Wait
Pause and wait with a gentle, expectant presence. Count silently to 10.
Weeks 1–4: this may not work often yet — that is okay.
Weeks 5–8: aim to start here most of the time.
Weeks 9–12: wait up to 30 seconds, only if she is calm and engaged.
Step 2
Visual
Tap the relevant word or picture on the core board without asking her to copy. Wait 5 more seconds.
💡 Tap "more" and pause.
Step 3
Choice
Offer two clear options. Hold up both, use visuals, or show one in each hand.
💡 "More or finished?"   💡 "Slime or bubbles?"
Step 4
Model
Say the phrase naturally, then wait. Do not ask her to repeat it. Then help her access what she was communicating about.
💡 "I want slime."   💡 "More please."   💡 "Open."
Tracking: Note which step you used in the activity log each time. Over 12 weeks, the aim is to see whether she begins to communicate with less support.

Key Word Sign (KWS)

Key Word Sign means signing the most important word in a sentence while you speak. It is not full sign language. It can help your child understand and express words, especially when she is finding speech difficult in the moment.

Priority signs to learn with your speech pathologist: more, stop, finished, help, I want, yes, no.

Your speech pathologist will show you these signs in person. Free resources are also available at keystrokesign.com.au to support your practice at home once you have learnt the signs in session.

Supporting emotions at home

A guide for acknowledging feelings — an important part of helping your child communicate and feel safe.

Why acknowledging emotions helps communication

When children feel heard, they are more likely to become calmer. When calm, children may find it easier to communicate. Your child is still developing language for emotions — words like "I'm frustrated" or "I feel overwhelmed" may not yet be in her toolkit. When you name feelings for her, you are teaching her that vocabulary and building her trust that she can come to you when upset.

Research on emotion coaching and co-regulation shows that acknowledging the feeling first, before guiding the behaviour, can help children calm and build their emotional language over time. This is not about accepting all behaviour — it is about separating the feeling, which is always valid, from the reaction, which can be gently guided.

The key difference

Responses that can unintentionally shut down feelings:
"Stop crying." / "Don't be sad." / "Smile!" / "You're okay, stop it."
These responses often come from wanting to help, but they can leave a child feeling unheard or confused about their own feelings.
Acknowledging and guiding:
"I can see you feel angry. That makes sense. Let's take a breath together."
"You look sad. It's okay to feel sad. I'm right here."
"I know you don't want to stop. That's frustrating. Let's do two more minutes."
The feeling is named and accepted. The next step is offered calmly.

In the moment — what to do when she is upset

When she is upset, start with Regulate, then Relate, then Reason. This means: help her body feel safe first, connect with her second, and only teach or practise language once she is calm. The Three Rs framework recommends supporting regulation before expecting reflection, learning, or problem-solving.

1
Regulate first — help reduce overwhelm before expecting communication.
Use fewer words. Reduce noise, lights, crowding, and demands where possible. Offer familiar calming supports, such as a timer, countdown, quiet space, comfort item, movement, deep pressure, or sensory tool.
Try not to ask questions, explain, correct, or teach while she is distressed.
2
Acknowledge simply — name what is happening in a short, concrete way.
You might say: "You wanted more." "Video stopped." "That was hard." "You're upset." "I'm here."
If emotion words like angry, sad, or frustrated are not meaningful for her yet, use simpler language linked to the situation: "All done video." "You wanted more." "Hard." — Say it once or twice, then pause. You do not need to repeat, explain, or make her respond.
3
Stay close and calm — your calm presence can help her feel safer.
Use a soft voice, slow movements, and relaxed body language. Stay nearby if she wants you close, but give space if closeness increases her distress. Do not force eye contact, touch, talking, apologies, or "calm down" behaviours.
This is the Relate step: connecting through a safe, attuned, and sensitive relationship.
4
Use her known co-regulation tools.
This might include: counting together, slow breathing beside her, a timer countdown, headphones, a quiet space, squeezing a cushion, movement, gentle facial compression only if she seeks it and it is calming for her.
Sensory supports should be offered, not forced. What calms one child may overwhelm another.
5
Wait until she is settled.
Do not rush. Full calm can take several minutes, and for some children it may take much longer. Being present and co-regulating is enough. You do not need to "fix" the emotion or turn the moment into a lesson.
6
Once calm, briefly model the language.
Only when she is settled and able to take in language, offer one short model. For example:
"You wanted more. More please."   "Video stopped. All done."   "You felt upset. Help."   "Next time, you can point here for more."
Keep it very short — one sentence is enough. This is the Reason step: teaching, reflecting, or practising only after regulation and connection have happened first.
Important: If she is still distressed in any way, skip this step entirely. Just doing Regulate and Relate is still successful. Over time, this builds her trust that big feelings are safe, adults will help her through them, and communication can be supported once her body is ready.

Based on The Three Rs: Regulate → Relate → Reason. Beacon House (2019)

When your child is upset or dysregulated

Co-regulation is the process of using a calm, predictable, and supportive presence to help your child feel safer and move toward regulation. When someone is overwhelmed or dysregulated, their ability to process spoken language, problem-solve, make decisions, or respond flexibly may be reduced. The goal is not to control their emotions, but to reduce demands, increase felt safety, and support their regulation until they are able to recover.

Step by step — supporting co-regulation

1
Regulate yourself first.
Before addressing your child's distress, check your own tone, pace, posture, and emotional state. Autistic people may be highly sensitive to changes in voice, facial expression, movement, urgency, or tension.
Take a few slow breaths, soften your voice, drop your shoulders, and ground yourself. Your calm, steady presence helps reduce pressure and supports safety.
2
Assess and manage the environment.
Dysregulation is often linked to sensory overload, emotional overwhelm, uncertainty, communication difficulty, pain, fatigue, hunger, or unmet needs.
Reduce sensory input where possible — dim lights, lower noise, reduce crowding, pause demands, offer headphones, provide access to comfort items, or move to a quieter space if she wants that.
3
Minimise and simplify language.
During distress, spoken language may be harder to process. Questions, lectures, corrections, or commands like "calm down" can increase overload.
Use minimal, slow, clear language. Try short reassuring phrases such as: "You're safe," "I'm here," "No rush," "Take your time," or "I can help." Visuals, gestures, or silence may be more helpful than speech.
4
Provide empathic presence and respectful boundaries.
Co-regulation often means being nearby without demanding eye contact, conversation, explanation, or immediate behavioural change.
Stay present, but respect personal space. Do not force touch, eye contact, apologies, or verbal responses. Offer choices where possible: "Space or company?" "Lights off?" "Headphones?" Validate the emotion without escalating the situation.
5
Offer sensory regulation, if safe and welcome.
Sensory input can help some autistic people regulate, but it must be individualised and consent-based. What calms one person may overwhelm another.
Offer, rather than impose, options such as a weighted blanket, deep pressure, a cushion to squeeze, cold water, movement, wall push-ups, rocking, stimming, or a quiet space. Breathing exercises can help some children, but model them gently rather than demanding them.
6
Bridge to self-regulation once calm.
Reflection should happen only after she is fully regulated. Trying to teach, analyse, or problem-solve during distress can feel overwhelming.
Later, review what helped using a low-pressure approach. You might use an "I feel / I need" chart, a sensory menu, visuals, or a simple conversation about what to try next time. Co-regulation builds trust, self-awareness, and future regulation skills over time.
Note about sensory behaviours: Your child's sensory seeking (facial pressure, vocal stimming, mouthing, small objects) is an important part of how she regulates. If you notice these increasing significantly or causing safety concerns, please mention it to her OT at the next appointment.

Words explained

Simple explanations for the speech pathology terms used in this program.

Echolalia
Repeating words or phrases she has heard before — from you, from videos, from earlier conversations. This is a normal and important part of language development for many autistic children. It is not something to stop.
Gestalt language processing
A way of learning language in whole chunks or scripts (called gestalts) rather than word by word. Very common in autistic people. Over time, children break these chunks apart and build more flexible language.
Natural Language Acquisition (NLA)
A framework developed by Marge Blanc that describes how children who learn language in chunks naturally develop toward flexible, spontaneous language — in stages. This program is based on this framework.
Gestalt / script
A whole learnt phrase used as one unit — for example, "Amma I want gimme please" used for all requests. These are real communication. Over time, the goal is to help them become more flexible and varied.
Spontaneous language
When your child says something without being prompted or asked to copy. This is what the program is working toward — her choosing to communicate on her own, in her own words.
Modelling
Saying the phrase you want her to learn — naturally and in context — without asking her to repeat it. The goal is for her to hear the language and use it in her own time.
Core words
A small set of words that work in many situations — like "more," "stop," "I want," "finished." These are the building blocks of flexible communication and are on her core word board.
Prompting hierarchy
A step-by-step guide for how much help to give — starting with the least (just waiting) and only adding more if needed. Over 12 weeks, the goal is to need less prompting over time.
Dysregulated
When a child's nervous system is overwhelmed — they may cry, stim more, or shut down. It is not bad behaviour. It means she needs support to feel calm again before communication can happen.
Co-regulation
When a calm adult helps a child calm down by staying calm themselves and using gentle tools like counting, breathing, or physical closeness. You are already doing this — it is one of the most important things you can do.
AAC
Augmentative and Alternative Communication — tools that support communication alongside speech, such as picture boards, signs, or apps. Research shows AAC does not stop speech from developing — it can support it.
Key Word Sign (KWS)
Signing the most important word in a sentence while you speak. Not full sign language — just the key word. Useful when speech is harder, such as during upset or sensory overload. Learn these with your speech pathologist first.
Generalising
Using a skill in new places, situations, or with new people — not just where it was first practised. For example, using "I want" at school or with grandparents, not just at home. This is the final stage of any new skill.
